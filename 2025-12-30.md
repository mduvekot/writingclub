OK, then. Let's say you can ignore al ethical aspects of a tool, how it was created, what it requires, who benefits from its use. How DO you want to deal with the ethics of its use? 

You could to ask questions, like: Who is being harmed by its use, but not its existence?

You're going to have to separate use from existence. Is that possible? Let's take a guillotine, for example. 

One interesting property of guillotines is their very narrow use case. There is really only one thing you can use them for.  Now compare guillotines with hammers, which arguably have many affordances, and notice that  ethical neutrality requires that at least one of those affordances is benign use. It is has not beneficial uses, it probably should not exist. And this is very true of hammers, hammers are perfectly usable in ways that cause no harm and have benefits. 

But this is not true of all tools, and to extend ethical neutrality to tools that do not have such affordances would be a mistake. They only tools that can be used in a way that is ethically neutral are tools that have such possibilities. Guillotines have no such possibilities, they offer no benign use at all. Therefore, all use of guillotines is unethical. 

But providing benefits is not enough to make a tool ethically neutral/benign. A tool also needs to not cause harm when it is in use. For example, if we have a machine that does something that benefits me (let's say a car, to drive me to work), but that also (by necessity) emits toxic particulate matter when I use it, it's use is no longer ethically neutral. So ethical use of a tool has an additional requirement to benign affordances, it must also not have harmful requirements (toxic emissions, waste). I may be able to ignore those, but other people (children with Astma, say) have no choice in the matter. If I choose to use my car for my benefits, and they're experiencing the harm, they ought to have the ability to regulate my use by requiring that my car meets emission standards, for example. Additionally, my car requires the existence of infrastructure, roads, gas stations. Such externalities can be more harmful than the car itself (extraction of of fossil fuels). When use of the car requires roads, roads are also "in use". 

To summarize,  a tool is ethically neutral only if
1. Its use is optional
2. It affords (some, at least one) beneficial use
3. Its operation can be regulated
4. Its operation does not rely on harmful externalities

We haven't even touched on non-use yet. 

A hammer that is not in use is hardly ever a problem. But a car that's not in use, and let's admit it; that's almost all of the time, is large enough that the amount of space it takes up becomes a problem, it is ugly, obstructive, hot when left in the sun, even dangerous and makes that space unavailable for other, more productive or fun uses.   

A rake is only a neutral tool if it can be stored safely when not is use, in a shed, for example. If I leave it in a a public park, it becomes a safety hazard. 

So, we'll add a fifth requirement
5. It can be safely stored or removed from shared space when not in use

Does this apply to generative AI? 
1. It's use is not optional.
2. The benefits are dubious, but lets assume a 20% gain in coding speed
3. The AI industry has resisted, and continues to resist, regulation
4. The externalities are harmful
5. It cannot be removed from public space.
