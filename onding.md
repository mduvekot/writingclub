I read your article about tools with much interest. It made me want to think about tools and user a bit more. Here are some of the unstructured ideas your article inspired:

I suspect that we can gain some clarity in our thinking about these things if we expand our vocabulary from tool and user to include device, apparatus, machine, operator, programmer.

Not every thing that aids in a task is a tool. Musicians and scientists call them instruments. Instruments are different from ordinary tools in that they are resonant. I know, so are hammers, that is why we had to invent dead-blow hammers that are not. I say theire resonant because the result of the process manifests in the instrument, not in an artifact. 

A machine is  an embodied process.

Computers are (disembodied) universal machines.

Software operates on abstract representations of things, not the thing itelsef. The chamfer tool in a CAD application operates on a model of a thing, it is equivalent to but different from a router with a bevel bit or  a table saw. The woodworker has a relationship to the piece and the tools that the user-or more precisely, the operator-of the CAD software, who is insulated from the consequences of her use. The virtualization removes responsability, there is no chance of injury, risk of ruining the piece or the tools. The operator is alienated from the work, as it were.
