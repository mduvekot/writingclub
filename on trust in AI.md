
I keep hearing that we need to be able to trust things; our government quite a bit, but mostly we're being asked to trust AI, or told that for AI to become accepted by the general public, we need to feel that AI is trustworthy. We need to "Foster trust (in AI) through operational transparency", "Lack of trust in AI tools is a significant factor inhibiting deeper AI adoption". "The characteristics of trustworthy AI are integrated into organizational policies, processes, and procedures."[AI_RMF_playbook]()

The point seems to be that unless people trust AI, they won't use it, and we **must** get them to use it, so we have to them them to trust it. Not by changing AI, necessarily, but by changing the minds of its potential users. 

If I a __trustor__, place my trust in another person to do what they said they will do, I make myself vulnerable to the __trustee__, and surrender control over the outcome based on assumptions I make about the motivations, honesty, fairness, or benevolence of the other party. 

Bluntly put:  You can only trust someone who has something to loose that they care about. Money, reputation, status, their freedom. You would (should) not trust someone who has "nothing to loose". I might trust someone because I can infer from clues I have about trustee's membership of professional organizations that have standards for ethical behaviour, their license, reputation, etc.. 

These do not and cannot exist with AI. AIs not only do not have a code of ethics, they cannot be held accountable for violations of such a code. Let's say an AI does something that is in violation of such a hypothetical code of ethics. How are we going to sanction that AI? By withdrawing it license? What license? By deleting it?  

AIs don't have motivations. Unless told what to do, they do nothing. They certainly claim to be helpful (which is a bit different from benevolent_: "I'm here to help you..." but they don't know what that means, so they'll also "help" me eat glass or pebbles.  

AIs cannot be honest, because they hallucinate and have no capability for introspection

AIs cannot be fair, because their training data is so biased.

AIs cannot be benevolent, because the disposition to do good requires will, intention, which they lack. 

It follows then, that there is no basis for trusting an AI, hence AIs cannot and should not be trusted. QED

# but what if...
we didn't really mean trust in the sense of human to human interrelations, but confidence that a taks assigned to an AI will be executed competently? 

We could reframe that as "Lack of ~~trust~~ _confidence_ in AI tools is a significant factor inhibiting deeper AI adoption". Should we have confidence? Do we feel that AIs are _reliable_?

Reliability requires consistency, which a stochastic system lacks, and the ability to function without failure, (ie. it cannot be wrong, which AIs frequently are).  So, also no. In fact, AI has demonstrated its unreliability, so frequently that it is demonstrably  un-reliable. To deny that is to deny reality. To say that "It will get better..." is an admission that it is currently insufficient. 
# so ...
AI can only be used if, *a)* a human assumes responsibility for it's output *b)* a human assumes accountability for its inconsistencies, inaccuracies and failures. *c)* it is restricted to operating in a an environment where no harm to non-AIs (humans, animals, the environment, anthing/ or anyone one that has a stake, interest or moral standing) can occur.  That is, it shall never be permitted to make decisions that involve such entities.
