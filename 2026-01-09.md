Noticing anthropomorphizing in our discussions on tool use is just as important as ntoocing ot in discussions on AI. So-called "AI", after all, is "just a tool". Or so they say. 

"A more deliberate and thoughtful way forward is to talk about “AI” systems in terms of _what we use systems to do_, often specifying input and/or output. That is, talk about functionalities that serve our purposes, rather than “capabilities” of the system. Rather than saying a model is “good at” something (suggesting the model has skills) we can talk about what it is “good for”. Who is using the model to do something, and what are they using it to do?"

**Who is using the model to do something, and what are they using it to do?**

That's a good question, because it yields meaningful answers.

Addition questions we might ask : Who controls the model? Who benefits from its use? What are its inputs/operands? What are its outputs? What functionality does it have? What does it afford? What affordances does it offer? Who collected the data that the model was trained on? 

To say or imply that a model/tool has skills is to confuse the tool with its user.

Our intuition would object to the claim that an articulated welding robot is a skilled welder, even though such a robot is capable of "accomplishing something" with precision and certainty. 

The welding robot is capable of performing welding, making a weld, but we do not recognize it as being "skilled at welding". So merely accomplishing a weld with precision and certainty is insufficient to capture our intuition/concept of skill.

This unskillfulness of the robot becomes apparent when the conditions under which the robot operates change. It has been configured, with great precision, to execute a pre-determined process under conditions with very narrow tolerances.  Should those conditions change, the robot will fail, and will be unable to recover from that failure. A skilled welder, however, is able to recognize such changing conditions, adapt to those changes or recover form failure by making changes to the process, using their understanding of context, domain knowledge and experience. A skilled user has high resilience to variation/uncertainty/change.

Skilled users are to able to recognize, interpret, generalize and adapt to observed patterns that are completely novel. Our definition of skill should capture adaptivity, generalization, resilience, and context sensitivity. 

**Skill is the capacity to perceive patterns (including novel ones), interpret their significance, generalize from limited experience, and adapt one’s actions to maintain effective performance under changing or uncertain conditions.**

To say that an LLM "is good at" something (for example, writing hymns) is to attribute it a skill it does not possess. In stead we could say: "The LLM is able to generate text output that resembles the surface form of a hymn". Or if we want to be very technical about it: 

The LLM performs probabilistic next‑token prediction conditioned on an input sequence and computes a distribution over possible continuations and samples from it to produce a sequence of tokens and then implements a statistical mapping from input text to likely output text based on patterns in its training data, thereby encoding statistical regularities of language, including grammar, syntax, and common discourse patterns, to approximate linguistic structures that are represented in its training distribution. It maintains local coherence and grammaticality through learned token‑to‑token dependencies to produce a sequence of tokens whose surface form resembles texts labeled or recognized by humans as hymns by matching the stylistic, structural, and lexical patterns associated with hymns in its training data, resulting in a statistically plausible imitation of the hymn’s observable features.

So instead of "The LLM is skilled at writing hymns", we say:

**The LLM produces hymn‑like outputs without any underlying identity or internal model of the genre.**

or 

**The LLM produces text whose statistical properties approximate those of the genre as represented in its training data.**

or 

**The LLM produces text with corpus‑congruent patterns.**

This appears to be problematic in the context of using LLMs  to generate *code*. How can it be that if the above is true, the LLMs produce functioning code that works?

That is because code is different from literary text. In what way? Because code has no content, it only needs to be grammatical. Code doesn't understand what it operates on. Of course, a function may expect a string as an argument, but as long as it is formatted correctly as a string, the code  will work. 

For code, This means that **surface‑level statistical regularities are unusually predictive**.
