A model is a proxy/representation/stand-in that behaves/produces outputs similar to-like the thing it models. 

Maybe this is better? _A model is a representation that allows reasoning about its target system._

We can model things or systems or phenomena , and make predictions about how things will behave based on the behavior of the model that we have built. For example, a model of the weather can predict the behaviour of a local weather system. It can tell us if it will rain tomorrow. In the model <-> thing-that-is -modelled relation, what is a term for the thing-that-is -modelled? That is not entity or system, but more like sign <-> referent. Target system? 

I want to distinguish between _producing similar output_ and _operating in the same way_. A fluid  can be described using Navier-Stokes equations but fluids don't perform those equations. Some models are analogous; they not only produce  similar output, they operate in the same way, others do not, and are hence ontologically distinct from analogous models. So we have representational and analogical models or perhaps surrogate and homologous models. Then there's isomorphic modes that share the structure in addition to the output, but do not operate in the same way; they use a different mechanism.

The tools themselves become like the thing they work/operate on at the ontological level. 
The ruler tool in photoshop behaves as a ruler, but it uses a different mechanism, it is performing an arithmetic operation (diff) on two vectors (two points, represented as x,y coordinates in a Cartesian coordinate system that is used to model an image). As the target system (the image) is digitized, the tool must follow, or I'd be sitting here using a plastic ruler on my screen and noting the millimetres between two points. 

What also changes is the kind/type of relationship between the tool and it's thing/material. It will shift from a material relationship between things to a semiotic relationship between concepts.

We might say that: _A tool inherits the ontology of the network/domain it operates in, because it is defined by its relations._ Ref.: Heidegger(1957) , Gilbert Simondon (1958, [_On the Mode of Existence of Technical Objects_](https://www.sfu.ca/~andrewf/simondon(1).pdf)) Ian Hacking (1983, [_Representing and Intervening: Introductory Topics in the Philosophy of Natural Science_](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/representing-and-intervening/**F6506B708BB5A8B6A5D884BDCF28E7B7**)) and Bruno Latour (1987, [_Science in Action_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_in_Action_(book)))

What operates on "world models", isn't that what we call cognition? Being able to get around in the world? Cognition is a tool for getting around or survival. 

What happens then when something (a tool) operates on symbols? The use of the symbols requires that certain rules are in place in order to work (have meaning). Some forms are meaningful , like a+b, other are not like  a=\*.  The tool has to use the same rules as the stuff. It has to be able to take an expression and turn it into a a structure. That's how we get grammar and syntax. To make meaning is to apply the grammar and syntax correctly.
