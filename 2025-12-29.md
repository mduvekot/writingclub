
# Camera and the City
## Date
Until March 15th, 2026
## Location
National Gallery of Canada

AS IDEA
In the city, conceptual photographers engage with the street
as a way to explore the built environment and the many fea-
tures and possibilities of the photographic medium. As cities
grow and transform, the street becomes a place to reflect
on architectural development and renewal, the volume of ad-
vertising in public and private spaces, and the overwhelming
presence of consumer culture. In many of these works, the
street is more than just a backdrop - it serves as a tool to bet-
ter understand the potential of photography and our individual
relationships to the spaces we inhabit.


So, they're saying:

The street serves as a tool to better understand the potential of photography and our individual relationships to the spaces we inhabit.

and

The street serves as a tool to better understand our individual relationships to the spaces we inhabit.


The form of those statements is akin to 

a tool is a device (screwdriver) that allows performing an operation (turning) on an operand ( a screw)

so we get:

The device (street) allows performing an operation (understanding) on an operand (the potential of photography).

The device (street) allows performing an operation (understanding) on an operand (our individual relationships to the spaces we inhabit).

consider the aspectual class (per Zeno Vendler) of the verbs: **understand**, like **know** is a _state_, not an _activity_; something you can do to an operand.  

Tools require either activities or accomplishments, but do not use states and achievements. For example, an accomplishment is : "use a brush to _paint_ a picture". An activity is  "hammer". 

But "understand" is static and does not have an endpoint, which makes it a *state*. "Notice" has an endpoint and occurs instantaneously, which make it an "achievement".   

The sentence commits a category error by treating _understanding_ — a stative predicate — as if it were an operational process that a tool could enable. Tools require dynamic predicates (activities or accomplishments), not stative ones.
