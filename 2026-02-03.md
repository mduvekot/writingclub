Where Newton refers to induction, is he referring to what we now call mathematical induction (which is a form of deductive reasoning) , or what we now call inductive reasoning, i.e,. generalization, causal inference? 

Newton is referring to empirircal induction, reasoning from particulars to generals.

a hypothesis (a conjecture as they (Mathematicians) call it) is a logical statement for which
neither a proof nor a counterexample has yet been found.

For physicists a hypothesis is the conceptual assumption of particular phenomena, formulated in the language of mathematics.

there may (or most probably will) exist a superior theory: one that addresses more phenomena and/or needs fewer hypotheses. In fact, this methodology is just a reformulation of the well-known, very useful scientific principle called Occam's razor: *entia non sunt multiplicanda praeter necessitatem.*

What imposes an impenetrable barrier to our visualisation capabilities is no great challenge for mathematical formalism: conjectures and theorems frequently operate with multi-dimensional abstract objects, embedded in vector-spaces of arbitrary or even infinite dimension. 


modern science has successfully abandoned the intuitive viewpoint of natural philosophy; visualisation is no longer an inherent part of scientific methodology

Schinner is not saying that Pre-Newton, diagrammatic reasoning might have been a thing, but that has since been replaced by (Newton's) inductive method..., but that we no longer require depictions of processes to reason about phenomena. (because we can use mathematics)

It is the tragedy of contemporary science that this strict separation of hypothesis from theory, and of theory from visualisation (as demanded by Isaac Newton) is weakening again, even among some scientists, though more frequently in the public view represented by modern mass media.
