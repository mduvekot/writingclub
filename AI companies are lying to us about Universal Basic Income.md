### Why a cold ecological calculus leaves the working class to die

[](https://substack.com/@akhilpuri)

[Akhil](https://substack.com/@akhilpuri)

Nov 27, 2025

_Note: This essay explores scenarios of massive suffering. It isn’t meant to be fatalistic, and I discuss potential solutions at the end, but it is stark and unflinching about a scenario that isn’t covered in the mainstream media._

AI and robotics companies will have us believe that massive job losses from automation won’t be a concern because we will have such abundance that everyone can be given a Universal Basic Income (UBI). They claim it will be [large enough not just for basic needs, but for a life of luxury](https://officechai.com/ai/ai-wont-just-lead-to-universal-basic-income-but-universal-high-income-elon-musk/), spent creating art and pursuing our highest calling. %%but not just that, we will ALL be artists, and all we have to do is speak our ideas %%

This is a dangerous fantasy at best and an outright lie at worst. %%show, don't tell%% The default %%alternatives?%%  trajectory we are on is that people made obsolete by automation are likely to be left to die, while a narrow slice of the population prospers. %%why do you start with the concusion?%%

To understand why this path might play out, we need to establish a few things:
1. The shifting balance of power, which gives the AI/robotics companies the ability to dictate our fate %%that's a bit much%%
2. A cold incentive calculus borne out of our ecological crises that gives them a motive for letting us perish %%not sure I understand%%
3. A worldview rooted in the primacy of power and competition, coupled with the tragedy of the commons that traps even those with good intentions %%the tragedy of the commons is a bit out of place here%%  

This trajectory of untold suffering is not yet inevitable. But averting it requires us to first stare, unflinchingly, into the calculus that makes it the default path. Only then can we build the necessary leverage to change it. %%it is the _calculus_ that does that?%%

We begin by understanding the current balance of power.
## The balance of power

We live in a capitalistic society where the individual and corporate incentive is profit maximisation, not human well-being. The latest wave of AI has been unleashed by [corporations that have unabashedly stolen intellectual property to train LLMs](https://iapp.org/news/a/fair-use-or-free-ride-the-fight-over-ai-training-and-us-copyright-law). Many of these firms are controlled by individuals who resist higher taxation at every step. But they want us to believe they will suddenly work in the collective’s interest later. %%they might believe that they *do* work in the collective interest (to save humanity, go to the stars) %% If they disregard any interest but their own now, why would they care about collective welfare when their power is absolute? 

They won’t. But the story of UBI remains ubiquitous because it is upheld by five different groups, each with its own reasons.

Many AI researchers are simply focused on the technical puzzle and its infinite promise, not the societal incentives surrounding it. Millions outside tech absorb the narrative because it offers comfort in a world they cannot influence. Some leaders in AI genuinely want a good future, but find themselves trapped by game-theoretic pressures—believing they must stay the course because their competitors will not pause. The UBI story helps them sleep at night. Others are indifferent; they assume their wealth will insulate them regardless of what happens. And a small group of malicious actors who use the promise of abundance to conceal a grab for absolute control.

Blindness, denial, tragic constraints, indifference, and malice—all converging to sell the same fantasy.

But no matter the motive, the **societal** **function** of the lie remains the same—keep unrest at bay. Power is already deeply concentrated in megacorporations, but the masses still hold more power collectively. Push people far enough, and they revolt—bringing the whole system crashing down. So the masses must be appeased, for now.

## The redundancy of the masses

The power of the masses comes from three things:

1. The ability to vote for change, protest or overthrow governments
2. The ability to boycott companies (if alternatives exist)
3. The fact that the masses are needed to perform the labour that gives corporations their power.

Once AI and robotics become advanced enough, this power balance will shatter. This will happen in four primary ways: human obsoletion through automation, compromised sensemaking through misinformation, hijacking of public institutions and surveillance-driven suppression.

All of these dynamics are already visible today. We only need to extrapolate a little to see where they lead.

Fake news, [concentrated media ownership](https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2023/1/6/big-money-is-choking-indias-free-press) and engagement-driven social platforms systematically distort public understanding of reality. The most insidious example is, of course, Facebook, which has faced multiple accusations ranging from [selling targeted advertising that influenced the 2016 US elections](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2018/mar/21/mark-zuckerberg-response-facebook-cambridge-analytica) to [amplifying genocide in Myanmar](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2021/dec/06/rohingya-sue-facebook-myanmar-genocide-us-uk-legal-action-social-media-violence).

AI-powered surveillance, [algorithmic throttling and bot armies](https://freedomhouse.org/report/freedom-net/2017/manipulating-social-media-undermine-democracy) are being deployed to monitor and deter dissent, making it increasingly dangerous to coordinate any action against oppressive policies. Warnings over these trends have been mounting for years. From [Wikileaks chronicling the CIA’s mass surveillance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vault_7) to [Sir Tim Berners-Lee, the inventor of the World Wide Web, raising concerns about the future of democracy](https://www.usatoday.com/story/tech/news/2017/03/11/world-wide-webs-inventor-warns-s-peril/99005906/) in 2017. The worst of these warnings is already coming to pass—a [2025 report from V-dem found that 72% of the world](https://www.democracywithoutborders.org/36317/autocracies-outnumber-democracies-for-the-first-time-in-20-years-v-dem/) no longer lives in a real democracy.

Meanwhile, well-funded lobbyists ensure that climate, financial and tech regulation continues to protect incumbent corporate interests, even when that directly conflicts with ecological stability or public well-being.

These dynamics are bad enough, but it’s automation that can shut the trap completely. Recent advancements make it clear that obsoletion in many sectors is now a matter of _when_, not _if._ [Internal documents from Amazon reveal plans to replace 600,000 jobs with robots](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/10/21/technology/inside-amazons-plans-to-replace-workers-with-robots.html). While [automation in China is leading to the rise of dark factories](https://www.youtube.com/shorts/HJL23CwrjGg)—factories so advanced that they don’t even need to keep the lights on because robots don’t need them. Not to mention the [50% of white-collar entry-level jobs that Anthropic CEO Darius Amodei says will be wiped out in the next 2-5 years.](https://www.axios.com/2025/05/28/ai-jobs-white-collar-unemployment-anthropic)[1](https://akhilpuri.substack.com/p/ai-companies-are-lying-to-us-about#footnote-1-180116158)

At this point, some people make misguided arguments to dismiss this fear as a spectre. _“_W_e will simply find new jobs for humans to do. The same argument of obsolescence was made at the time of the Industrial Revolution, and look how well that turned out for us.”_ This ignores three simple facts:

1. The rate of change is exponentially higher at this moment compared to the Industrial Revolution. %%Do we have numbers?%%
2. Nobody even knows what these new jobs would be, let alone creating them to match the rate of job loss or upskilling people fast enough to fill them.
3. This change is qualitatively different from what happened at the time of the Industrial Revolution. We are not simply creating tools that make humans more efficient. We are creating tools that can do **everything** a human can, but faster, cheaper and better. 

It is important to note that we don’t need to have 100% automation for this to be problematic. If 20% of labour gets automated, then 20% of people fall through the cracks. %%that doesn't follow%% If 80% of labour gets automated, then 80% of people fall through the cracks. %%neither does that%%  Our current system’s failure to support individuals with severe disabilities shows that this is likely to be the case. %%Granted, we have always treated prople with disabilities poorly%% [A 2024 UN report on ‘Disability and Development’](https://social.desa.un.org/sites/default/files/publications/2024-06/Final-UN-DDR-2024-Executive%20Summary.pdf) shows that those who cannot conform to the relentless demand for productivity are simply left to fend for themselves. 

Once we conclude that people will have no value as labour, the next fallback position people usually adopt is—_”But even the powerful need consumers for their products. The rich need the poor to grow their wealth. We won’t be redundant and powerless because they need a market!”_ This, too, is a relic of an old paradigm. %%permanent indebteness is a very attractive prospect for capitalists though, as it allows for continued extraction and accumulation of wealth even when the wealth of the endebted has been exhausted%% 

Money just represents optionality. %%I don;t know what the means%% It is a claim on real resources and services. Why do you need money or human consumers when you have robots to harvest natural resources and serve your every need directly? The masses become economically redundant. If a market or human service providers are needed, those can be maintained by having a far smaller population of say 500 million to 1 billion as opposed to the 8.2 billion today. %%The wealthy are advocating for making more (white) babies though%%

One might be tempted to say that—surely when humans see this playing out in the future, they will mount a resistance and seize power back from the corporates. %%Look at the history iof the oppresson of the Luddite resistance. Not sure  we have the appetite for such brutality anymore though. Those were differrnt times%% But remember that democracy is in decline, while AI-driven misinformation and surveillance will make it difficult to mount a resistance. If a small resistance movement somehow forms, it can be crushed ruthlessly with drones or military robots. [The British Empire had fewer than 100,000 troops controlling 350 million Indians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Raj). The ratio of controller to controlled is about to become vastly more efficient. It is why [Elon Musk is seeking a trillion-dollar pay package to have ‘strong influence’ over what he calls ‘Tesla’s robot army’](https://mashable.com/article/elon-musk-trillion-robot-army-tesla). His choice of words is revelatory and worth focusing on. 

The variability in potential future levels of automation or the time it takes to go from 20% automation to 80% automation creates a window where UBI might materialise for a few years. %%Huh?%% This would be because it would take time for the robot army to become large and capable enough to crush dissent. %%If you assume that automation outpacesdevelopments of robot armies, which is self-contradictory%% Lest that UBI lull us into complacency against the future threat, we must remember that what is being built is a private army. %%is it private if the state pays for it?%%

## The dark ecological calculus

We have now established the powerlessness of the masses and the ability of the megacorporations to treat us as they will. %%I'm not on board. We do have power, but it's weird power. %% We must now ask why this would result in them letting us perish. Perhaps the corporations will be benevolent? And even if they aren’t, maybe they will just let us be?

The intention and motive are interwoven pieces of the puzzle. To begin, let us look at how the shifting power balance paves the way for a dark ecological calculus to play out. %%I don't know w what ecological calculus is%%

We are in a period of ecological overshoot—consuming resources faster than Earth can replenish. %%Note: We cannot replenish Helium, or Lithium. Minerals don't grow on trees. %% 

The Earth’s massive scale, resource surplus and self-correcting ecosystem hid this fact for decades. %%Mothe rnatire might decide to kill us to restore balance.%% But our exponential%%please stop using that word%%  population, technological and consumption growth have ploughed through these buffers. %%if you mean exceeded capacity, say so%% 

The result %%of what? capitalism?%%— a complex, multi-faceted ecological crisis. Climate change is its most well-known manifestation. But [we have also breached 6 other planetary boundaries](https://www.stockholmresilience.org/news--events/general-news/2025-09-24-seven-of-nine-planetary-boundaries-now-breached.html)—critical ecological thresholds which ensure human civilisation remains viable. These include things like freshwater depletion, ocean acidification, soil degradation, plastic pollution and more.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Ji_a!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fedb1efb4-16be-447a-a535-abe222623199_1247x1172.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Ji_a!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fedb1efb4-16be-447a-a535-abe222623199_1247x1172.png)

Solutions to climate change often advocate for green growth, while ignoring these other boundaries. %%solutions don;t advocate%%  But even on climate change, if we think the green energy transition will save us, we need to consider the following arguments:

1. Most countries’ policies are nowhere on track to scale up green energy as fast as combating climate change, [decline in other energy sources](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QdfwH4LvTUs) or growth in energy usage demand. The US, of course, has taken a decidedly anti-green energy turn. [Current climate policies put us on track for 3 degrees of warming by the end of the century](https://www.politico.eu/article/united-nations-emissions-gap-global-warming-data-climate-change-report/). That’s double the 1.5-degree Paris agreement target, which was considered a red line for our safety. Wildfires and floods from [LA](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/california-wildfires) to [Canada](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/toronto/ontario-wild-fire-season-worse-than-10-year-average-9.6964765) to [India](https://wmo.int/media/news/devastating-floods-highlight-need-and-challenges-warnings) and more are already showing the results of crossing that red line. %%Still doesn't mean we shouldn't make that transition ASAP%%    
2. [We aren’t scaling up mining fast enough to have all the materials needed for the green transition in time](https://www.iea.org/reports/the-role-of-critical-minerals-in-clean-energy-transitions/executive-summary)

Of course, techno optimists will dismiss this view. They will point to a future with superintelligence that will remove all bottlenecks, abundant resources through asteroid mining, colonisation of the cosmos and more. For them, we need to wait only till the end of the decade, and our AI god will take care of everything. These arguments are directionally right about the radical potential of AI. All of those things can certainly happen. But these arguments ignore the fact that even with AI and robotics, it takes time for physical infrastructure to scale and transition. Ignoring these constraints is an appeal to magical thinking.

The argument that AI will solve our ecological crises has another critical flaw. It assumes that we don’t currently have enough ingenuity and resources to solve the challenges without AI. That it is somehow a technological issue. It isn’t. It is an issue of power and political will. If even 5% of the world’s GDP, i.e. 5 trillion dollars, were invested towards addressing our ecological crises, we would avert the worst of them. Instead, [we spend 1.5 trillion dollars a year subsidising fossil fuels](https://ourworldindata.org/how-much-subsidies-fossil-fuels).

Operating under our growth at all costs paradigm, with wasteful and unequal infrastructure, will ensure our life-sustaining systems continue to degrade until they fail catastrophically. How wise is it to wish away hard physical constraints, miscategorise our political challenges as technological and depend on an AI god that we don’t even know will act in the interest of mankind? A good life for all is possible. The green transition, too, is possible. But it requires a massive reorganisation of society to strengthen democracy and live within the limits of our planet. Think direct democracy institutions like citizens’ assemblies—bodies comprising regular citizens, picked at random, to decide policy. Think life configured around public transportation, walking and cycling instead of cars. Think tool libraries and equipment sharing instead of each person buying everything. Think healthcare focused on well-being instead of making money off sick people. An economy that focuses on human and planetary flourishing instead of profit maximisation through socialising harms and privatising gains. Tech that works for people and not against them.

This would also require the powerful to give up some of their current monetary advantage. Not out of charity, but out of the recognition that our current system is exploitative and leading to collective ruin. Some, like billionaire Mackenzie Scott and others who have signed the Giving Pledge, recognise where we are headed and are doing all they can to facilitate this shift. But some others would rather let people perish instead of giving up their power.

## A collision of worldviews and incentives

It is worth asking why they would choose that path. The answer lies in worldviews and more game theory.

There are two primary worldviews at odds in today’s world. One says:

> _“Humans are the masters of the Universe. Life is a competition. The strong take what they can, the weak suffer what they must. Power is the only truth worth worshipping.”_

The other says:

> _“We are a part of the interconnected, interdependent web of life. I only thrive when the rest of life thrives. Any damage to the whole diminishes me, just as any damage to me diminishes the whole. That it is a deep, sacred connection to ourselves, each other, nature and the metaphysical that lends life its richness and meaning”._[2](https://akhilpuri.substack.com/p/ai-companies-are-lying-to-us-about#footnote-2-180116158)

Those holding the first worldview misunderstand Darwin and think that people perishing in such an environment is just the nature of life. [They don’t realise that survival of the fittest in our context means cooperation, not competition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_selection). For them, the calculus arising from ecological considerations is simple. The truly dark implication of this worldview is that the masses won’t necessarily be left to perish out of malice. They will be left to perish out of indifference—an externality to the calculus of power.

But the true trap is even more insidious still. It ensnares even those who hold the second worldview. If you are willing to give up power to ensure planetary flourishing, but you think others won’t, you are going to be too afraid to act alone. You would think you are weakening your chances of survival for nothing. You would be tragically forced to act against your own values because you cannot trust others to cooperate.

So no matter your worldview, this game theory leads to even AI leaders wanting safe AI to race full throttle, [billionaires building bunkers](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cly17834524o) and making plans to escape to Mars instead of maximising chances of survival here on Earth.

And the same dynamic paralyses action at a nation-state level. If you, as a country, choose to be peaceful, but your neighbour is violent, then you have no option but to militarise or develop AI and robotics even while sacrificing your population. This is the dynamic at play in the US-China AI arms race.

The situation is compounded by the fact that a small but influential percentage of those upholding the status quo are not just constrained by game theory; they are malicious individuals with low levels of empathy and self-centred worldviews—often shaped by trauma or genetic predisposition.

Coming up against this deadlock of rational incentives, worldviews, cold ecological calculus and fear meeting fear at scale, our default trajectory does not look good. The ability, motive and intention for letting the masses perish all exist.

As the wise Charlie Munger said:

> _”Show me the incentive, and I will show you the outcome.”_

So where do we go from here? The central flaw in the cold calculus above is the belief that a privileged few can thrive on a planet in collapse. Yet, as the game theory reveals, seeing that flaw is not enough to escape the trap. We cannot rely on mass enlightenment, trust, courage or virtue alone to overcome the forces at play here.

The good news is that this future isn’t set in stone. It is the default path, but we have a few years[3](https://akhilpuri.substack.com/p/ai-companies-are-lying-to-us-about#footnote-3-180116158) before AI and robotics reach a level where this scenario can play out.

And if we act today, we can find ways out.

## Breaking the Deadlock: A Path Forward

To succeed, we need to change the power balance and the incentive landscape. I present one possible way below. It requires two foundational pillars:

1. Strong, corruption-resistant national democracies with decentralised power (e.g., citizens’ assemblies)
    
2. A multinational democratic coalition with enough technological and economic power to act as a third force against megacorporation oligopoly and the US-China AI/robotics duopoly.
    

The first greatly blunts the impact of corporate lobbying and takes away power from corrupt career politicians. It does so by divesting more & more power to local levels of government and incorporating direct democracy elements like citizens’ assemblies. [Paris and Ireland have already used these models to formulate policy on issues like climate change and abortion](https://isps.yale.edu/news/blog/2024/04/governing-citizens%E2%80%99-assemblies-lessons-from-france-and-beyond). Implemented at scale, this decentralisation of power will make the system resistant to hijacking and force transparency that will eviscerate corruption. It will enable the enactment of citizen-friendly policies at a nation-state level—ensuring we aren’t sacrificed at the altar of profit maximisation and game theory.

For the second— imagine a coalition of the EU, Africa, the rest of the Americas, Asia, and Australia. It would have the spending power and talent to create a formidable, purposeful AI and robotics program.

This coalition could:

- **Drain power from incumbent players by attracting top talent:** A top AI/robotics engineer today is complicit in their own doom by working for players with misaligned incentives. This offers them a purposeful alternative. If our best and brightest refuse to play ball, then the AI companies automatically lose power.
    
- **Force cooperation:** Control enough of the AI supply chain to force the US and China to the table to implement trustless, transparent cooperation mechanisms. This can end the AI arms race and increase the chances of AI alignment. [See the work of Yoshua Bengio, another Godfather of AI, for an example of how this could work](https://yoshuabengio.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/FlexHEG-Memo_August-2024.pdf)
    
- **Force the real debate on the desirability of AI and robotics:** The energy footprint of AI is set to be massive—[OpenAI wants to consume electricity equal to the whole of India.](https://www.businesskorea.co.kr/news/articleView.html?idxno=253470) Can we afford this while in an ecological crisis?
    

This solution is, of course, at a very high level and would need to be sketched out in far more detail. But it isn’t impossible. [The EU is already planning a 30 billion dollar investment in AI to catch up to China and the US](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/eu-plans-30-billion-investment-143047554.html). And there are multiple proposals exploring how an AI coalition could be governed, drawing inspiration from successful joint initiatives like CERN (physics and nuclear research) and [Intelsat](https://www.forethought.org/research/intelsat-as-a-model-for-international-agi-governance) (global satellite communication).

And you might disagree with this solution altogether, but the solution is not the point. We can think of other ones. The key point is that the dangerous lie of UBI will persist and take us to our doom until democracy is strengthened, incentives are realigned, and worldviews that recognise our interdependence become dominant. And we—especially those in tech—will be complicit in our own annihilation.

To those employed by these companies, I know you are not engaged in conscious deception. I know you genuinely believe in your mission and want to do good. When you talk of UBI and a future of abundance, I know you mean it. But I urge you to consider the systemic incentives your creations will unleash and the harm already being caused by them—[lest you end up like Geoffrey Hinton, lamenting your life’s work](https://economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/new-updates/i-should-have-godfather-of-ai-geoffrey-hinton-whose-research-helped-machines-learn-shares-why-he-regrets-now/articleshow/122993909.cms?from=mdr). You can and must choose a different path. The world is relying on you.

To the leaders of these corporations, especially those engaged in this cold calculus borne out of fear and trauma, I ask you to thoroughly consider how the future will play out. The path chosen by you is perilous. Your actions hasten civilizational collapse, and even your robot army is unlikely to prove equal to the task of containing the ecological forces you will unleash. And long before that, unaligned AI might treat you as disposable just as you treat the masses. You and your loved ones might perish in the very flames that you light. And if you somehow manage to survive and achieve your robotic kingdom, imagine what you would be the lords of. A cold, sterile world, built on the corpses of billions.

If you leave a few hundred million alive, what would your relationship to them be? Not one of connection, but of containment. You will be the warden of a global prison, forever paranoid, forever watching the gates. Who will you trust when your power is absolute? Every friend is a potential usurper; every kindness suspect. Forever isolated, forever alone, starved of beauty, intimacy and love. Will your soul be able to survive paying that price?

The voice that drives you to seek total control is lying to you. It promises safety, but it delivers solitary confinement. Redemption is possible. Liberation is possible. You can change course and be the hero in this story instead of the villain. I need you to believe that. The world needs you to believe that.

The time for fear-based politics and passive hope is over. A world where all of life can thrive is possible, but it won’t be handed to us. It must be built by us. The burden of this task doesn’t fall on the tech industry alone. It falls on all of us.

Reject the dangerous lie of UBI. Support democratic renewal. Advocate for transnational coalitions. Redirect your skills to work on human and planetary flourishing. Time is short. And you are needed.
