"Now researchers at the University of California San Diego have unveiled a new technology with the power to see cells in unprecedented detail. The tool, called fluorogen-activating coincidence encounter sensing (FACES), was developed in Associate Professor of Biochemistry & Molecular Biophysics Itay Budin's lab. https://phys.org/news/2025-10-tool-enables-nanoscale-visualization-lipid.html

Let's ask the same question again, what does this tool do, and to what does it do it? 

FACES uses engineered proteins and dyes to convert nanoscale biological encounters into fluorescent signals that can be imaged with a microscope.

We might say that the FACES tool is a system that involves resources/materials (dye), a process (sequence of actions by the lab technician) and functions that the cell itself performs according to a set of instructions.

In contrast to tools like screwdrivers, the FACES tool requires a lab technician to assemble the tool, so their actions are not "use", like that of a carpenter and her screwdriver, but "construction" or "assembly". This tool is not "ready for use", but has to be built each time it is deployed. 

Once assembled though, FACES requires no interaction with  a user, it functions autonomously.

Is a thermostat a useful analogy? We should look into thermostats as tools. Thermostats also function autonomously, once they're configured and turned on.

I'd say that a thermostat operates on sense-data, or signals, and automates the decision-making process. Is there a perhaps a whole class of such signal-driven autonomous tools? 

A toilet tank is an autonomous, signal‑driven tool  with a sensor (the float), a control rule(“if water is low, open valve"), an actuator (the valve) as it continuously regulates the tank’s water level.
