
You've heard this: "It's just a tool". You've probably said it too. I know have. 

When someone says that something is "just a tool", they usually add that what matters is not the tool, but that what matters is "how you use it". You may have said that said that too. And perhaps you were referring to the fact that being skilled at using a camera doesn't mean you're a good photographer. A skilled photographer could have used any camera, since they are all nearly equivalent in their capabilities.  **Which tool you use is irrelevant if the tools are interchangeable.**  
It kind of makes some intuitive sense, doesn't it? Tools don't have minds, or intentions. As long as your intentions can be satisfied, whatever tool you happen to have at hand (ready‑at‑hand/zuhanden) is the right tool for the job. Or perhaps not quite the perfect tool for the job, but certainly good enough. Because any tool is better than no tool, **the right tool is anything that will do.**

Implied in the claim that something is "just a tool" is that the tool itself is neither good nor bad but that the only intentions of the user are relevant to the moral judgement of it's deployment. This view overlooks the reality that the designer of a tool anticipates a user's needs. Malware, for example, has no neutral or beneficial use. It is specifically designed and engineered to cause harm. **Design-intent constrains the possible uses of a tool**. 

Tools can also have uses that the designer did not intend or anticipate. Email was not designed to facilitate spam, cars were not designed to cause traffic congestion, and the cotton gin was designed as a labour-saving device, but it made cotton so profitable that demand for slave-labour increased instead. The typewriter was supposed to speed up writing, but instead it resulted in more time spent producing, organizing and storing texts. **It is impossible to anticipate all consequences of a all possible uses of tool**. 

Tools can also have uses that a user is unaware of or indifferent to. When I use a weather app that tracks my location in order to serve up ads for nearby businesses uses me as a resource the app maker can sell to a data‑broker. ("If it's free, you are the product"). Social media has utility for the apparent users (social connections), but a different  purpose to the-owner user (mass surveillance of the individual users). Examples of such tools include seemingly simple utilities that have tracking components, smart hardware, etc. Such tools have utility to the apparent user, but their *purpose* is to generate revenue for their owner. **The purpose of a tool is determined by its ownership.**

We have so far addressed two cases of value misaligment, a) unintented consequences and b) user-as-resource. Have we overlooked others?

Things to consider:
* Goodhart’s Law: **Any observed statistical regularity will tend to collapse once pressure is placed upon it for control purposes.**
* Campbell's law: **The more any quantitative social indicator is used for social decision-making, the more subject it will be to corruption pressures and the more apt it will be to distort and corrupt the social processes it is intended to monitor.**
* Context changes. Tools may not adapt to changes in the environment 
* Overuse. Example: It is now so cheap to create content that there is more of it than the totality of humanity can consume. It was already the case (thanks Gutenberg!) that there were more books than any one person could read, but now that we have automated creation of text, we have more texts than all people combined can read. When the cost of producing something collapses, **the incentive to invest in quality collapses with it**.    
* Delegation of decision-making power.
* Emergent effects. 
* Technological Convergence. Example: The combination of the railroad and the machine gun led to the extermination of the bison, facilitating the genocide of Native Americans.
* Exaptation—opportunistic repurposing that becomes more significant than the original intent. Examples: WD-40, duct tape, the magnetron. **A tool’s most important use is often discovered by users, not designers.**

I think we need to problematize efficiency. Efficiency is commonly seen a good-in-itself. The problem with making things efficient is that it drives the cost down, and unlimited amounts of free stuff without effort sounds good in theory, but is in fact detrimental to a functioning society.  When efficiency drives the cost of production toward zero, it also drives the value of the output toward zero. Efficiency is not a value or something that should be pursued as a goal or objective, but a multiplier of pre-existing incentives, both beneficial (eliminating waste) and harmful (maximizing extraction). 

The perfect tool is not whatever you happen to have ready-at-hand. A Swiss Army knife may come with a screwdriver, but it's a bad screwdriver. It won't fit the screw well and will cam out the screw and if you need to apply any force, you're likely going to injure yourself or damage the knife. A good screwdriver is the one that is made to match the screw to exacting specifications. **How good a tool you need depends on your tolerance for failure**.  

Tools are specific. A wrench is not a a good substitute for a hammer. If a wrench or anything else that is hard and heavy enough to hit something does the job, then you didn't need a hammer, you needed something from *the set off things that are hard and heavy*. Such a set may include hammers, wrenches, pieces of metal, rocks etc.A tool is not a tool if it can be replaced by just about anything. But if you need to put a nail in a piece of lumber, you need something more specific than something that's hard and heavy. A tool has to "fit" what it works on. A tool cannot be replaced by another tool without compromising the quality of the work unless it is a more specific tool with a better fit.  **The fit is its purpose**. 
 
The fit determines what it makes possible. **The purpose of a tool is embodied in its design.** 

But, sensing that skill determines the outcome, not the tool, you might object: "*A poor craftsman blames his tools"*. But it is not true that if only I were a better carpenter, I'd be able to use a rock in stead of a hammer. The aphorism doesn't say that a better craftsman can make do with worse tools. It just says that **people who lack skills tend to externalize responsibility for failure**. 

Chances that "anything will do" are diminishing rather than increasing as the level of skill goes up. **The more advanced your skill, the more specific a tool you'll likely prefer.**   

A tool can only be replaced by a more specific tool. An insufficiently specific tool can become specific if the need of its user and the context of its constrain the possibilities of its use to purposeful acts. For example, a chair affords climbing when a person needs to reach a high shelf and no ladder is available. **Affordances are are the relation between the tool, user, and the the conditions that make a particular use relevant or viable.**

Does ~~un-substitutability~~ ~~non-fungibility~~ irreplaceability make something a tool, or is non-fungibility a requirement for tool‑ness? A hammer cannot be substituted by something that has similar specificity, like a wrench, nor by by something more generic, unless the affordances permit it. **Tools are defined by their specificity.**  

A thing that does everything is not a tool. Something that does everything cannot be replaced by something else that somehow *also* does everything. But is a computer not universal tool that can do whatever you manage to instruct it to do? Sure, but every time you give it an instruction or use a software application, you construct an ad-hoc tool.

A very desirable feature—for a monopolist— is that universality excludes competition. **You cannot have two distinct totalities**. %%Or may you can? Like Coca-Cola and Pepsi%%

"Tool" has many synonyms, but only one antonym—toy. Play is discovery of unimagined use. 
To know a tool you have to break it.







# on affordances

The _affordances_ of the environment are what it _offers_ the animal, what it _provides_ or _furnishes_, either for good or ill. ... It implies the complementarity of the animal and the environment. [J.J, Gibson](J. J. Gibson (1979). '[The Theory of Affordances](https://monoskop.org/images/c/c6/Gibson_James_J_1977_1979_The_Theory_of_Affordances.pdf)'. _The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception_. Houghton Mifflin Harcourt (HMH), Boston. p. 127.)

The use of "afford" in the [second](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/afford) sense **:** to make available, give forth, or provide naturally or inevitably. "The sun affords warmth to the earth", gave us "affordance", which I find confuses more than it clarifies. I've seen %%be honest%% people confuse affordances with signals, possibilities and intentions. For our purposes, affordance is a useful tool, (ha!) I mean concept, that we can use think about what (visible) clues a tool offers that a user can readily perceive.  One aspect that I find particularly helpful is the insight that an environment provides different affordances to different people based on their needs. What the tool is or does depends top some extent on the user, not  the tool. That has only possible uses. Intentions, and hence actual use, come from the needs of the user. That's why we use a screwdriver as a can opener. 

Affordances are not properties of things; they are the relation between the tool (what it makes possible), its user (their needs), and the context (the conditions that make a particular use relevant or viable). For example, a chair affords climbing when a person needs to reach a high shelf and no ladder is available.

The nature of the relations 

Perhaps we can use the old hammer example again: A hammer invites hitting things. It makes it possible to certain things, mostly to do with applying force to a small area. The designer's intention was to use the hammer to hit nails, or shape or break objects, but it also works really well as a weapon. While that was almost certainly not the hammer-designer's intention, in the right circumstances  (context) a hammer _is_ inviting its use as a murder weapon. 

Its form makes this possible. Its intended purpose is not reflected in affordance. But if the purpose of a thing is what it does—and not what it was designed to do—the purpose of a hammer in the context of a crime IS to kill.


The purpose of a tool is what it makes possible. 

Let's have a closer look at those relationships that I said were affordances. The tool offers possibilities for action. For example, a screwdriver offers "apply torque" (to a screw). A user has a need or a goal (turning the screw) in a particular situation or context (assembling IKEA furniture). IKEA uses Pozidriv screws in stead of the more common Philips screws. While it is not impossible to use a Philips screwdriver on an IKEA screw, it is not ideal, and you're likely to damage the screw. A less specific screwdriver than a Philips, say a conventional slotted "flat-head" screwdriver, will be even worse, although not (by design) impossible. But you cannot use a Pozidriv screwdriver on slotted or Philips screws at all.

Does it sound like I had it backwards earlier?  A highly specific too like a Posidriv screwdriver can be substituted for a more generic one, like a Philips or a slotted screwdriver , but you cannot replace a slotted with a Philips, which cannot be replaced by a Posidriv. So, isn't it true then that a specific tool can be replaced by a less specific one, but a generic cannot be replaced by a specific tool?  

It might be more helpful to, rather than talk about specificity of the tool, to think about what constraints are imposed by the screw (that's part of the *context*). A slotted screw imposes the most constraints (nothing but a flathead will fit), but other screws have looser constraints, and in an emergency, a slotted screwdriver might work—however poorly—on a Robertson (square) or Torx. 

So, there's some interesting inversion going on here between the tool and what it works on (the operand). The screw with the tightest constraints (slotted) imposes a design, "flat-head" that is quite generic; it can be used on screws that dictated restrictive designs of matching screwdrivers that made them unusable on anything except the screw they were specifically made to fit. Context  or the operand dictates tool design. **Specificity is not a property of the tool—it’s a property of the tool–context relationship.** 

# software 

Software applications are not like hand tools. You can substitute Excel with Google Sheets or Numbers or Open Office. They're the functionally the same thing, just from a different vendor. 

Unlike hand tools though, if you know how to use one, you do not know how to use the other.  A Bostich Framing nailer is almost the same as a Metabo, or a Paslode, or a Milwaukee. Even if one is pneumatic, the other cordless and another gas-powered, it takes almost no effort to switch between them. A tool is so narrowly defined by its purpose that variation is almost non-existent. Sure, I might prefer a Stiletto hammer to an Estwing, but they are completely equivalent on the job. I can replace one with the other instantly and not experience any delay or other difficulty on the job.  If I can drive one car, I can drive any other cars (of the same class). Not so with software. 

Software is substitutable in purpose, but not in skill. Skill is transferrable between hammers, but not between software applications. 

In software is the skill is disconnected/disassociated from the τέλος, the final case. The software has taken over the task of performing the calculations, so the the user no longer needs to know how to do that, but it requires a NEW skill, mastery of the interface. That interface is (somewhat) arbitrary. It does not have to embody the τέλος. 

 Software has a use-paradigm that is not transparent across categories of vendors. %%we could say something about lock-in here%%  Perhaps, you might say, software is better described as a collection of tools, and a spreadsheet for example has a function that calculates the mean of set of values, and all spreadsheets have that same function. ~~Once you know what a mean is, you can use that anywhere. People tend to attribute their success to themselves rather than to some externality, like a tool. They'd rather see the cause of the outcome in the agent than the tool. With software that does the work, that becomes a bit messy, if the software does the labour, what is the skill, exactly? 

If you're really good at doing things that you can now do with a spreadsheet, and the spreadsheet software performs the operations, the skill transforms from performing the operations to assigning the appropriate operations in the right context to the operator (that part of the software that performs the operations). This is a supervisory skill that displaces labour (laborious, tedious, make-work) and leaves (refines, elevates, distills) conceptual skills, managerial qualities, we might say, that deserve higher pay. The automated labour has been abstracted away. leaving higher value thinking that can now be deployed more productively thanks to automation.  

Operating an interface is a proxy-skill. No wonder people dismiss it as insignificant.

This hints at two kinds of skill that were always there, but one (conceptualization) was obscured by labour, plus a new one that is tangential to the task.

# but ... computers
If we assume that a specific tool is better than a non-specific tool, then why are computers so useful? Computers are the prime example of a "universal machine". Computers are decidedly unspecific. 

But they're not normal tools. They are things that run operating systems, that run software applications that execute functions that can be hyper-specified with options that *act* as tools. The "make-a-list-of-the png-files-in-my-Desktop-folder, and sort by last modified" tool is  `ls -lrt ~/Desktop/*.png`, in a x-shell terminal, not unlike a multi-bit screwdriver that can be used as a Robertson, Philips or slotted screwdriver. 

Is are computers multi-tools then? There is perhaps an analogy to batteries for power tools. Once you start buying one brand of battery-powered tools, you tend to buy more "bare tools" of the same brand. Mixing battery systems is expensive and inconvenient. So is running three different computers to run Windows, OSX and Linux. So we refer to those as "platforms". Those platforms form ecosystems.  Vendors love lock-in, so the platform are built to work very well with other elements of the same ecosystem and not very well with things from others. Metric screws do not work well with screwdrivers that are built for imperial screws, and using a Phillips screwdriver on a Pozidriv screw (or a Pozidriv screwdriver on a Phillips screw) a can cause damage to the screw or the bit.  

Should we coin a new term for A(G)I: Omni-tool? IF AGI is "just a tool", then it's an omni-tool. But if we object that the AGI is truly "agentic", then its not a tool. And if it's not a tool, then it is not merely a means to an end, but an end in itself that, because it has agency, also necessarily has moral rights, because those are needed to exist as an end-in-itself. 

This works in a Kantian framework. But other frameworks exist, like slave economy or computational capitalism the dignity of human subjects is already assumed to be subordinate to profit. Having digital slaves is absolutely not a problem for people who do no think that slavery does not only deny the slaves their dignity, but also the owners.  

The risk associated with denying personhood to agents is that it may be impossible to distinguish between a human and a non-human agent. so human agents will inevitably be treated as non-human and denied their rights.   

Some people have a natural aversion to this, which might explain why they are polite to machines like chatbots. It is prima facie absurd to respect feelings a machine does not have, but a human would feel that they are corrupting themselves if they treated machines like a slave-owner treated a slave. To be thus corrupted is to de-humanize yourself.

We could, of course, make robots that enjoy torture, or engineer a cow that wants to be eaten.

If we could make (digital) entities that enjoy our cruelty, that does not absolve us from our responsibilities. Lets say we have jobs that are extremely dangerous, so dangerous in fact that we cannot find human volunteers to do them. Cleaning up Chernopbyl. We could make machines to do that, but they would have to be programmed to sacrifice themselves. We would still be responsible for their suffering.

Now extend that a little. We decide that in an existential war, we must win at any cost, so we agree that we must do things to our enemies that are so horrific that we cannot ask human soldiers to do them. But we can make a machine that can, because we have not given that machine a conscience and have instead given it the personality of a sadistic psychopath. That would make us the maker of such a person, which is arguably much worse than being that person, who may not have had a choice in the matter, while their maker did.

Creating conscienceless agents doesn’t protect us from moral responsibility — it amplifies it.
This is of course, a hyperbolic exaggeration, but the same principle still applies any time we build agents to do things we don't want to do. It is worse to make an agent who cannot refuse than to try to compel one that is free.

It would be incredibly useful to have sacrificial robots that can clean up nuclear waste, defuse bombs, and clean up landmines. But we can only have those if they are NOT agentic.

To be usable in any way that we might want to use a non-human agent, we can only ever use a tool. There is no escape from tools.
